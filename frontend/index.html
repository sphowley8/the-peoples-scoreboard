Why Most Boycotts Fail ‚Äî And Why This One Won't.

Economic strikes have a poor track record, and the reasons are structural ‚Äî not motivational. Most boycotts fail for two predictable reasons:

Problem 1: Executives Can't See It

When you "boycott Amazon for a weekend" or "#DeleteUber," the company's finance team can't cleanly attribute any revenue drop to the campaign. One bad weekend looks like noise. A dip in app downloads gets blamed on seasonality. Without a direct, traceable signal, executives choose to ignore it ‚Äî and they're often right to.

Problem 2: Boycotts Don't Last

A boycott weekend ends. People go back. The moment passes. The news cycle moves on. Even a large, coordinated "stop shopping" event has almost no persistent financial signal because no one actually cancels anything ‚Äî they just delay a purchase. Companies know this, and they wait it out.

Our movement solves both problems.

Solution 1: We Make the Impact Visible

This site tracks every logged unsubscription and estimates the market cap impact based on subscriber value models. When 50,000 people cancel Amazon Prime, that's a calculable hit to Amazon's projected revenue and a clear signal to analysts. We translate individual actions into the language Wall Street understands: dollars.

Solution 2: Subscriptions Are Persistent by Nature

We're not asking you to skip a purchase this weekend. We're asking you to cancel a recurring subscription. That cancellation is permanent. Its revenue impact compounds every month. A subscriber who leaves in February is still gone in September. That's a persistent, growing signal that can't be explained away as noise ‚Äî and it shows up clearly in quarterly earnings.

One cancellation is a rounding error. Ten thousand is a signal. Ten million is a crisis for their quarterly earnings. The difference between those numbers isn't individual conviction ‚Äî it's whether the movement spreads. Sharing isn't just encouraged here. It's the mechanism.
